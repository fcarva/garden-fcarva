## Introduction

All right, well thank you so much Sam and Wendy for inviting me to participate in this panel with Philippe. You three are some of my intellectual heroes, so it's a real honor to be in conversation with you.

I'm a PhD candidate in the Economics department at Harvard, and my PhD focuses on microeconomic theory. So for the last few years, I've been very focused on the models side of "values, models, policies, and narratives." Today, I'll be picking up largely where Sam left off.

I really love this framework that Sam and Wendy have come up with, and what I want to do is illustrate the usefulness of this framework by using it to navigate through a discussion about one particular way that corporate governance—that is, firm decision making—might change in a new paradigm. In other words, I want to use this framework to try to connect some dots: what are the policies, models, and narratives around corporate governance in a paradigm based around voice and democratized market interactions?

### Economic democracy

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212171923.png]]

I think in some sense I'll be starting with a value that Sam talked about at length, although he didn't exactly use the term—the term appears in their paper. I would say that a big component of this new paradigm being described is something like **Economic Democracy**.

Going back to what the prior neoliberal way of thinking about things left out, it is that through framing economic interactions in a "morality-free zone," the term Economic Democracy was sort of an oxymoron. In some sense, there's nothing to democratize when we're in this morality-free zone. So one response to this is: how do we think about giving voice to various stakeholders in economic decision making?

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212171950.png]]

Shrinking Capitalism (PDF).

My definition of economic democracy is something like shifting decision-making power in firms away from managers and shareholders and toward stakeholders such as workers, consumers, suppliers, and communities more broadly.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212172540.png]]

### Corporate governance in a new paradigm

Naturally, given that this is sort of a question of control and authority and decision-making, it seems like corporate governance—the set of tools that we use to govern the firm—must be a key piece of an economic democracy-based paradigm. But how do we really think about what sorts of corporate governance policies most neatly fit into this paradigm?

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212172633.png]]

Today, people talk with a lot of romance about the idea of worker or consumer cooperatives. That would be a totally new way of organizing business. There are many countries around the world where there's some form of formal co-determination, like in Germany, France, and Japan, where workers have seats on the board and can participate in firm decision-making.

But what I see in a lot of the discussion today is a set of values based around economic democracy that are in search of an integrated set of models, policies, and narratives around corporate governance.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212172704.png]]

It's useful to go to the kind of neoliberal perspective to see how neatly that perspective came up with a way of talking about corporate governance. The corporate governance policies implied by this way of thinking are, on the one hand, **Shareholder Primacy**—that is, the only formal duty of the firm is to its shareholders. On the other hand, we have **Competition Policies**—regulators tasked with ensuring there is enough competition in a given market, identifying abuses of market power, and correcting them through structural remedies (like breakups) or behavioral remedies.

The narrative here is so simple. Fifty years ago, Milton Friedman said, "The social purpose of business is to increase profit." That is so easy to remember and a useful way of integrating all of these different levels.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212172835.png]]

### Business Roundtable shift

I want to say that this is not totally an abstract, academic attempt to come up with a connection between the dots of these values and policies. I'm sure many people here are aware of the 2019 statement by the Business Roundtable, where this group of business leaders moved away from the shareholder primacy paradigm—at least in their vernacular—to say, "We want to say that the purpose of the firm is to create value for its stakeholders."

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212173244.png]]

Now, not much has happened since 2019 as a result of this document, but people are certainly talking. Even if we want to be cynical about whether the business leaders really had intentions to change their policies, it's still a big move to see this grasping toward a new way of thinking about the social purpose of business.

Now, I think there is a set of values around economic democracy, and what that means for corporate governance really requires filling in these different levels that Sam and Wendy talk about.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212173653.png]]

### Facebook case (2008-2009)

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212173730.png]]

I want to interject with a really brief story, which is inspired and retold in a paper that lays out one particular antitrust case against Facebook. There's a really unusual moment in Facebook's history around 2008 and 2009.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212173850.png]]

At that time, Facebook had one of its very early privacy scandals. Facebook was still in a competitive market; it had competitors like MySpace. One thing that Facebook did to regain the trust of its user base after a major privacy scandal—and in order to stay competitive—was to affirm a commitment to privacy.

This was a statement in February 2009 by Mark Zuckerberg which accompanied a major policy change in which the company actually decided to create a system for user referenda, where users could vote on changes to the terms of use in the privacy policy, and that vote would be binding.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212173923.png]]

Mark Zuckerberg described what prompted this change, saying: _"People share more information... a new relationship is created by internet companies... companies like us need to develop new models of governance."_

Of course, we hear a lot of people talking about these things now, but this was 2009. And this was not because of pressure from the government; this was because of pressure from competition. This was because of pressure from MySpace and a desire to take control of this market.

So they set up these procedures. They set up direct voting, stating they would put changes to the terms of use to a vote and the results would be binding. They also set up a User Council that would have not just an advisory role, but actually a kind of formal authority in the decision-making process of the firm.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212174023.png]]

### Epilogue and lesson

Lots of people discussed ways in which this was a major move. But of course, we know what the epilogue to this story sounds like. Essentially, what happened was Facebook was no longer subject to competitive pressures. They vanquished their competition, and of course, they just did away with this user referendum process.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212174101.png]]

The reason why I spent precious time telling that story is because I think it points to an interesting lesson for us and an interesting model for thinking about what role democratic processes could play in a firm.

In some sense, **democratic processes can be a sort of remedy for market power**.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212174134.png]]

When we have firms that are natural monopolies, where their production processes have increasing returns to scale, or where the real issues are about platform economics which don't fit neatly into prior economic paradigms, we see that competition policy and shareholder primacy just aren't a solution to the abuses of monopolistic power in a lot of settings.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212174229.png]]

### Conclusion

The particular policy that I'll explain now to conclude is this: What if we think about changes to corporate governance—specifically stakeholder governance—as a kind of remedy to market power?

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212174305.png]]

Being very abstract, the idea here would be that when an abuse of market power is discovered by a regulator, the best answer might not be "break them up" or subject them to some behavioral remedy. Rather, the answer might be to oversee some kinds of democratic processes of control and create enforcement around democratizing the decision-making of the firm.

This could take forms that look a lot like co-determination with users, workers, and suppliers, but it could also take other forms.

To bring back Sam and Wendy's pictures: I think this is one particular way in which, if we start from this "government and market" line, all we can see really is competition policy or antitrust as we typically think about it.

![[escrita/imagens/Pasted image 20251212174424.png]]

But once we start to pull off that line and think about civil society solutions, we can see that actually, stakeholder governance in some sense may be a kind of solution to certain forms of market power.
